Numerator Data Science Challenge¶

Christopher Ivanovich, Data Scientist @ Linkbal Japan¶

The Scenario:¶

You have been tasked by the Product team to develop a new type of analysis that can predict buyer behavior based on historical purchase activity. They want to create a simple model that can predict where shoppers are likely to visit, given a set of input variables. The outcome they would like to predict is the Retailer or set of Retailers where a shopper is most likely going to shop. The input variables can be any of the provided variables and / or any other derived features that explain the likelihood of retailer visits.

The model should be used to answer the following questions:

  1. Are there any additional input variables we could provide to improve your model? What are they, and why?
  2. What are the most/least important features that explain the likelihood of retailer visits?
  3. For Hispanic Walmart shoppers, which other retailers are they most likely to shop at?
  4. Among Dollar General shoppers, which shopper group(s) are most likely to visit, and how do they look different than other shopper group(s)?
  5. How would Safeway shoppers and/or other retailers be impacted if Safeway were to close their stores?

Some of these object columns we expect to be numerical or boolean, such as salestax and transcribed, can conveniently be recast. In the particular case of salestax, while its values are numerical in nature, they are discretely distributed (few current values, though in principle it could be continuous); treating it as numerical will save us the need to one-hot encode it.

Other columns can or should also be one-hot encoded, depending on whether or not they are not already binary in nature. This isn't strictly necessary for the model we will be using (random forest, perfect for a first sweep on a classification task), and in some cases it might degrade performance, but doing so will aide interpretability of the feature importances, and, given the large amount missing values for some columns, treating that as an indicator column as well will help us in our interpretation. Also, doing it here will save us the trouble of doing it later were we to evaluate different models for which it is required or preferred (like sequential neural networks and xgboost).

Questions¶

1. Are there any additional input variables we could provide to improve your model? What are they, and why?¶

As we see below, location is an extremely important feature, which is pretty intuitive, when a shopper determines which store they will be visiting, given that these are all traditional, brick-and-mortar realtors, and these shopping trips are almost all undertaken physically. So, for starters, we would do better to improve the completion of geographic feature data, as we note that store_geo_store_id is 0 54% of the time, telling us we don't have data for these entries. Luckily, we do have a large number of location features that likely serve to fill in the gaps for the model, but they too are frequently incomplete.

The importance of geographic data of both a store and a user is a no-brainer, people need to go to places within reasonable distance.

But we also see that purchase details like basket totals in dollars and quantities, sales tax, trip ratings, and payment methods (particularly cash) are of importance, as well as key demographic details, like age (being an adult and of prime working age), the presence of children, and gender (being female seems to give the model better power of separation). Having children but not having them on the trip may indicate the importance of the children being of school-attending age, and certainly older children require increased quantities of food and other goods.

These feature importances can loosely be interpreted as reflecting the model's ability to maximize the split at each step in the decision trees, so payment method = cash for instance might at some point reflect easy separability into more affordable versus more expensive stores (people are perhaps more likely to pay in cash versus other methods if they don't have a bank account, as an example of an interpretation that would need extensive supporting analysis).

We see above that the per-user average shopping trip and the distribution of basket counts paint a quite different picture of retailer preference on an individual basis -- the count of transactions is quite positively skewed, meaning a few people disproportionately account for large portions of the transactions data. Those users may shop disproportionately at a small subset of the available retailers, further biasing the dataset in favor of those retailers more than a per-user analysis would suggest is generalizable to our larger population of users.

Taking this observation into account, another way to have gone about this task would have been to limit each user to a fixed number of transactions, to improve generalizability and to reduce the impact of frequent shoppers, though this comes at the cost of using a reduced volume of data, and perhaps increasing the false positive rate for less frequently visited retailers. If the goal is to provide reliable predictions of the store a particular user is likely to attend, it is better to focus on maximizing precision rather than recall.

At first blush, these are not bad metrics. Accuracy is about 63%, and given that there are 35 classes, the probability of assigning one correctly by chance was 1/35 or about 3%.

My intuition is that precision is more important than recall, since it seems more important to me that we minimize getting a prediction wrong rather than maximize getting it right -- so in this case, it appears we are headed in the right direction.

However, if this were on the job, I would far more exhaustively compare different sampling approaches, models, and metrics, and confer with business teams to determine which metrics are the most appropriate for this particular problem and its business goals.

Last thoughts; Questions?¶

For next steps, things I would attempt are:

  1. Dealing with the imbalanced classes issue using one of the aforementioned methods.
  2. Re-evaluate my data handling to make sure there is not data leakage or misuse of a class, and double check that I am not spuriously removing some important feature.
  3. Check for multicolinearity if a model requires it, and remove sets of features with excessive collinearity (this is another reason why RFC is so useful for preliminary investigations).
  4. Make use of a validation set and not simply a test set.
  5. Use XGBoost, Catboost, and a neural network to observer performance differences.
  6. Choose the apparent winner and optimize that model with a randomized, possibly Bayesian, paramter search.
  7. Consider how best to deploy the final model and its requisite end-to-end infrastructure, ie:
    • Do we need to use distributed systems to process data and train the model?
    • Do we need to write some kind of API to receive inputs and return predictions in streams or batches? Or can we simply dump the output somewhere?
    • Does this warrant automated hyperparameter searches on a recurring basis, as well as automating retraining and testing of the model?
